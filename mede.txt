=== Question ===
Describe the pathophysiology of Type 1 vs Type 2 Diabetes.
=== Reference ===
Type 1 is an autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells (insulin deficiency). Type 2 involves peripheral insulin resistance followed by a gradual decline in insulin production.
=== AI Answer ===
Type 1 Diabetes: Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells → little to no insulin production. Leads to hyperglycemia, ketosis-prone. Requires lifelong exogenous insulin.

Type 2 Diabetes: Insulin resistance in peripheral tissues (muscle, liver, fat) + relative insulin deficiency. Initially, beta cells compensate by increasing insulin secretion, but over time they fail. Associated with obesity, inflammation, and genetic factors.
=== Question ===
What is a myocardial infarction, and what are its primary causes?
=== Reference ===
A heart attack. It occurs when blood flow to a part of the heart stops. It is primarily caused by the rupture of an atherosclerotic plaque leading to thrombosis in a coronary artery.
=== AI Answer ===
Myocardial infarction (heart attack): Death of heart muscle tissue due to prolonged ischemia (lack of oxygen). Usually results from acute thrombus formation over a ruptured atherosclerotic plaque.

Primary causes:

Atherosclerosis with plaque rupture

Coronary artery spasm

Thromboembolism

Rarely: vasospasm (Prinzmetal angina), spontaneous coronary artery dissection
=== Question ===
Explain the difference between a virus and a bacterium, and how their treatments differ.
=== Reference ===
Bacteria are living organisms treated with antibiotics. Viruses are infectious agents requiring a host to replicate, treated with antivirals or prevented with vaccines.
=== AI Answer ===
Bacteria: Single-celled prokaryotes, can survive independently, have cell wall, reproduce by binary fission. Many are susceptible to antibiotics.

Viruses: Non-living intracellular parasites, require host cells to replicate, composed of genetic material (DNA/RNA) + protein coat.

Treatment:

Bacterial infections → antibiotics (e.g., penicillins, macrolides)

Viral infections → antivirals (e.g., oseltamivir, acyclovir), often supportive care; vaccines for prevention
=== Question ===
What is the mechanism of action of SSRIs?
=== Reference ===
Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors block the reabsorption of the neurotransmitter serotonin in the brain, making more serotonin available to improve message transmission between neurons.
=== AI Answer ===
SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine, sertraline) block the serotonin transporter (SERT) on presynaptic neurons → inhibits reuptake of serotonin from the synaptic cleft → increases serotonin availability in postsynaptic receptors. This enhances serotonergic neurotransmission, which helps regulate mood, anxiety, and sleep.
=== Question ===
Describe the concept of herd immunity.
=== Reference ===
When a large portion of a community becomes immune to a disease, making the spread of the disease unlikely, thereby protecting those who cannot be immune (e.g., newborns).
=== AI Answer ===
Herd immunity occurs when a high percentage of a population becomes immune to an infectious disease (through vaccination or prior infection), reducing its spread. This protects unvaccinated or immunocompromised individuals by lowering the probability of contact with an infected person. Threshold depends on pathogen’s basic reproduction number (R₀). For highly contagious diseases (e.g., measles, R₀ ~12–18), ~95% immunity is needed.
